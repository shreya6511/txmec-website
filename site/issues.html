Issues

About HB 311:

The bill was written by Rep. Sheryl Cole (D) and Rep. Donna Howard (D), Rep. Ina Minjarez (D), Rep. Drew Springer Jr. (R), Rep. Gene Wu (D), and co-authored by Rep. Ray Lopez(D), and Rep. James White (R) It was introduced in the Texas House of Representatives on November 13, 2018. The last action on HB 311 was on May 1, 2019, and has since been left pending in committee. HB 311 covers feminine hygiene products which include: tampons, sanitary napkins, menstrual cups, menstrual sponges, menstrual pads and other items used for the purpose of hygiene with the menstrual cycle. With this bill, the sale of feminine hygiene products is exempted from sales taxes.

About the Tampon Tax:

In 30 states across the country, menstrual products (including pads, tampons and menstrual cups) are considered non-essential items, and are resultantly subject to states’ sales tax. Access to these products greatly affect a menstruators’: overall health and hygiene, ability to attend school, capacity to perform and attend work, ability to participate and function in everyday activities.

Despite the fact that these products are a necessity to menstruators, they are categorized as “luxury” items in states across the US, and thus taxed as such. This tax makes menstrual products difficult, sometimes even impossible, to afford for millions of menstruators. In Texas, there can be up to an 8.25% luxury tax.

• With a box of tampons costing at least $7.00 pre-tax, a single box of tampons can be up to $7.58 per box with the maximum sales tax applied.

• The minimum wage in Texas is $7.25, so for many of the menstruators in the low-wage workforce, they will spend well over an hour every month working just to be able to afford menstrual hygiene products.

• That estimate does not account for whether there are other family members who menstruate that need to be provided for or whether one box will provide the right number of tampons in the sizes the menstruator needs.

• According to the incoming data for the 2020 census in Texas, 14.9% of the population lives in poverty. The majority of menstruators will use an average of 9,120 tampons in their lifetime. These numbers lead to pervasive period poverty, or the inability to afford menstrual health products.

A recent study found that about one in five girls in the United States have either left or missed school because they didn’t have access to menstrual products. Not having access to menstrual products has resulted in:

• Students missing school and losing valuable time spent in the classroom

• Members of the workforce without paid sick leave missing days of work and not receiving pay, resulting in financial difficulties for those menstruators and their families

• A discriminatory barrier to education and work for those who menstruate and have limited access to menstrual products

• Severe threats to a menstruator’s health, such as toxic shock syndrome and dangerous infections from using unhygienic materials to manage their periods. These materials can be toilet paper, socks and old clothing, paper bags, grocery bags, and even cardboard.

• Discrimination against transgender men and nonbinary individuals who menstruate, especially considering about 16% of transgender men make less than $10,000 a year, and transgender individuals are twice as likely to experience homelessness. These conditions further limit access to menstrual products for transgender and nonbinary individuals.

Support for HB 311
Throughout Texas, numerous legislators, organizations, and individuals support the end of the taxation of menstrual products, also known as the Tampon Tax. There have been numerous bills introduced in both the Texas House and Senate to eliminate this tax, including:

• HB 232HB 219

Other States and Countries and the Tax
Fortunately, advocates across the country are rising up and demanding change. There has been a significant amount of progress in the last few years as advocates make their calls louder and hold lawmakers accountable, but the work is far from over. There are already 20 states that are tampon-tax free – Ohio and Washington removed theirs in March/April 2020 – and Texas needs to be the next one. Ireland, Canada, the United Kingdom, Kenya, India, Colombia, Australia, Germany, and Rwanda have all reduced or completely eliminated the tax on menstrual hygiene products since 2004.
